Nafissa Thompson-Spires’ epistolary masterpiece “Belles Lettres” explores middle-class African American academic identity through humor. The short story is made up of correspondences between two mothers and was a part of Heads of the Colored People, published in 2018. “Belles Lettres” uses comedy to explore identity and critique pretentiousness. The name of the story refers to people who used to write beautiful letters where great detail was applied to the language, grammar, and appearance of the letter. The name is the first joke of the story because it becomes quickly apparent to the reader that the letters convey an ugly fight, even whilst maintaining the illusion of a civilized exchange of letters.

The two mothers, Lucinda and Monica, each have a daughter in the same class in Westwood Primary School. Lucinda starts off the exchange of letters because “Fatima may have started a nasty rumor about my Christinia” (Thompson-Spires). Right off the bat, Thompson-Spires bombards the reader with various titles and accolades when Lucinda sings her letter with the following “Dr. Lucinda Johnston, PsyD / Licensed Therapist / Welcome Wagon, Westwood Primary School / Events Coordinator, Jack and Jill, Claremont Branch”. Such a lengthy and detailed signature is of course completely 0ut of place when written at the end of a note intended to reach another parent at the bottom of a child’s backpack.

Another source of baffled amusement for the reader is the perfect combination of formal with sassy. Monica, for example, has the following advice for Lucinda “I really hope that in addition to help for her lies and early signs of psychosis, you will get some help for her weight problem before she ends up—and I say this respectfully, so I hope you won’t be offended in the least—like you. Children do pick these things up from their mothers.” (Thompson-Spires). Such mean comments are present throughout the exchange and become meaner with each letter. What makes the meanness shocking, and yet hilarious, is the fact that the mothers’ daughters are used as ammunition and targets in their war of words.

The tone of the letters eventually becomes more ranting and unhinged, as the namecalling happens less through innuendo and more through direct slurs. After the police, Jesus, and a lawyer get mentioned there is a fistfight between the girls, and the mothers are invited to talk with Principal Lee. This is the point of the story where Nafissa Thompson-Spires delivers her final blow to the pretentious duel of the belletrists. After meeting the principal Lucinda and Monica evidently become fast friends and completely bury the hatchet. This sudden and ridiculous change in their relationship pulls the rug from under the reader who is ready for a sad or at least exciting climax. Instead, we get the comical cherry on top of the cake that is Thompson-Spires’ story “Belles Lettres”.

★★★★★

An epistolary short story to make you laugh out loud.
